## 14.3 Scattering from Layered Materials

> ## 14.3 分层材质的散射

In addition to describing scattering from larger-scale volumetric media like clouds or smoke, the equation of transfer can be used to model scattering at much smaller scales. The LayeredBxDF applies it to this task, implementing a reflection model that accounts for scattering from two interfaces that are represented by surfaces with independent BSDFs and with a medium between them. Monte Carlo can be applied to estimating the integrals that describe the aggregate scattering behavior, in a way similar to what is done in light transport algorithms. This approach is effectively the generalization of the technique used to sum up aggregate scattering from a pair of perfectly smooth dielectric interfaces in the [ThinDielectricBxDF](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models/Dielectric_BSDF.html#ThinDielectricBxDF) in Section [9.5.1](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models/Dielectric_BSDF.html#sec:thin-dielectric-bxdf).

> 除了描述来自较大尺度体积介质（如云或烟雾）的散射外，传输方程还可用于在更小尺度上建模散射。LayeredBxDF 将其应用于此任务，实现了一个考虑来自两个界面散射的反射模型，这两个界面由具有独立 BSDF 的表面表示，它们之间有介质。蒙特卡洛方法可以应用于估计描述聚合散射行为的积分，方式类似于光传输算法中所做的。

Modeling surface reflectance as a composition of layers makes it possible to describe a variety of surfaces that are not well modeled by the BSDFs presented in Chapter [9](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models.html#chap:reflection-models). For example, automobile paint generally has a smooth reflective “clear coat” layer applied on top of it; the overall appearance of the paint is determined by the combination of light scattering from the layer’s interface as well as light scattering from the paint. (See Figure [14.11](#fig:scattering-layered-surfaces).) Tarnished metal can be modeled by an underlying metal BRDF with a thin scattering medium on top of it; it is again the aggregate result of a variety of light scattering paths that determines the overall appearance of the surface.

[![](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Light_Transport_II_Volume_Rendering/pha14f11.svg)](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Light_Transport_II_Volume_Rendering/pha14f11.svg)

Figure 14.11: Scattering from Layered Surfaces. Surface reflection can be modeled with a series of layers, where each interface between media is represented with a BSDF and where the media between layers may itself both absorb and scatter light. The aggregate scattering from such a configuration can be determined by finding solutions to the equation of transfer.

With general layered media, light may exit the surface at a different point than that at which it entered it. The LayeredBxDF does not model this effect but instead assumes that light enters and exits at the same point on the surface. (As a [BxDF](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models/BSDF_Representation.html#BxDF), it is unable to express any other sort of scattering, anyway.) This is a reasonable approximation if the distance between the two interfaces is relatively small. This approximation makes it possible to use a simplified 1D version of the equation of transfer. After deriving this variant, we will show its application to evaluating and sampling such BSDFs.

### 14.3.2 Layered BxDF

The equation of transfer describes the equilibrium distribution of radiance, though our interest here is in evaluating and sampling the BSDF that represents all the scattering from the layered medium. Fortunately, these two things can be connected. If we would like to evaluate the BSDF for a pair of directions and, then we can define an incident radiance function from a virtual light source from as

([14.34](#eq:virtual-directional-light))

If a 1D medium is illuminated by such a light, then the outgoing radiance at the medium’s interface is equivalent to the value of the BSDF, (see Figure [14.15](#fig:layers-virtual-directional-light)). One way to understand why this is so is to consider using such a light with the surface reflection equation:

![](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Light_Transport_II_Volume_Rendering/pha14f15.png)

Figure 14.15: If a medium is illuminated with a virtual light source of the form of Equation ([14.34](#eq:virtual-directional-light)), then the radiance leaving the surface in the direction is equivalent to the layered surface’s BSDF,.

Thus, integrating the equation of transfer with such a light allows us to evaluate and sample the corresponding BSDF. However, this means that unlike all the [BxDF](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models/BSDF_Representation.html#BxDF) implementations from Chapter [9](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models.html#chap:reflection-models), the values that LayeredBxDF returns from methods like f() and PDF() are stochastic. This is perfectly fine in the context of all of pbrt ’s Monte Carlo–based techniques and does not affect the correctness of other estimators that use these values; it is purely one more source of error that can be controlled in a predictable way by increasing the number of samples.

The LayeredBxDF allows the specification of only two interfaces and a homogeneous participating medium between them. Figure [14.16](#fig:layered-bxdf-geometric-setting) illustrates the geometric setting. Surfaces with more layers can be modeled using a LayeredBxDF where one or both of its layers are themselves LayeredBxDF s. (An exercise at the end of the chapter discusses a more efficient approach for supporting additional layers.)

![](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Light_Transport_II_Volume_Rendering/pha14f16.png)

Figure 14.16: Geometric Setting for the LayeredBxDF. Scattering is specified by two interfaces with associated BSDFs where the bottom one is at and there is a medium of user-specified thickness between the two interfaces.

The types of BxDF s at both interfaces can be provided as template parameters. While the user of a LayeredBxDF is free to provide a [BxDF](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models/BSDF_Representation.html#BxDF) for both of these types (in which case pbrt ’s regular dynamic dispatch mechanism will be used), performance is better if they are specific [BxDF](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models/BSDF_Representation.html#BxDF) s and the compiler can generate a specialized implementation. This approach is used for the [CoatedDiffuseBxDF](#CoatedDiffuseBxDF) and the [CoatedConductorBxDF](#CoatedConductorBxDF) that are defined in Section [14.3.3](#sec:coated-bxdf-specializations). With this BSDF, layered materials can be specified as either one- or two-sided via the twoSided template parameter. If a material is one-sided, then the shape’s surface normal is used to determine which interface an incident ray enters. If it is in the same hemisphere as the surface normal, it enters the top interface and otherwise it enters the bottom. This configuration is especially useful when both interfaces are transmissive and have different BSDFs.

For two-sided materials, the ray always enters the top interface. This option is useful when the bottom interface is opaque as is the case with the [CoatedDiffuseBxDF](#CoatedDiffuseBxDF), for example. In this case, it is usually desirable for scattering from both layers to be included, no matter which side the ray intersects.

One way to handle these options in the f() method would be to negate both directions and make a recursive call to f() if points below the surface and the material is two-sided. However, that solution is not a good one for the GPU, where it is likely to introduce thread divergence. (This topic is discussed in more detail in Section [15.1.1](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Wavefront_Rendering_on_GPUs/Mapping_Path_Tracing_to_the_GPU.html#sec:basic-gpu-model).) Therefore, both directions are negated at the start of the method and no recursive call is made in this case, which gives an equivalent result.

At each vertex of the path, we will want to compute the incident illumination due to the light. As shown in Figure [14.20](#fig:layered-virtual-light-contribution), there are three factors in the light’s contribution: the value of the phase function or interface BSDF for a direction, the transmittance between the vertex and the exit interface, and the value of the interface’s BTDF for the direction from to.

At a path vertex, the contribution of the virtual light source is given by the product of the path throughput weight that accounts for previous scattering along the path, the scattering at the vertex, the transmittance to the exit interface, and the effect of the BTDF at the interface.

The LayeredBxDF implementation uses two of the three—sampling the phase function or BRDF at the current path vertex (as appropriate) and sampling the BTDF at the exit interface—and then weights the results using MIS.

#### BSDF Sampling

The implementation of Sample\_f() is generally similar to f(), so we will not include its implementation here, either. Its task is actually simpler: given the initial direction at one of the layer’s boundaries, it follows a random walk of scattering events through the layers and the medium, maintaining both the path throughput and the product of PDFs for each of the sampling decisions. When the random walk exits the medium, the outgoing direction is the sampled direction that is returned in the [BSDFSample](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models/BSDF_Representation.html#BSDFSample).

With this approach, it can be shown that the ratio of the path throughput to the PDF is equal to the ratio of the actual value of the BSDF and its PDF for the sampled direction (see the “Further Reading” section for details). Therefore, when the weighted path throughput is multiplied by the ratio of [BSDFSample::f](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models/BSDF_Representation.html#BSDFSample::f) and [BSDFSample::pdf](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models/BSDF_Representation.html#BSDFSample::pdf), the correct weighting term is applied. (Review, for example, the fragment << [Update path state variables after surface scattering](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Light_Transport_I_Surface_Reflection/A_Better_Path_Tracer.html#fragment-Updatepathstatevariablesaftersurfacescattering-0) >> in the [PathIntegrator](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Light_Transport_I_Surface_Reflection/A_Better_Path_Tracer.html#PathIntegrator).)

However, an implication of this is that the PDF value returned by Sample\_f() cannot be used to compute the multiple importance sampling weight if the sampled ray hits an emissive surface; in that case, an independent estimate of the PDF must be computed via a call to the PDF() method. The [BSDFSample::pdfIsProportional](https://pbr-book.org/4ed/Reflection_Models/BSDF_Representation.html#BSDFSample::pdfIsProportional) member variable flags this case and is set by Sample\_f() here.

### 14.3.3 Coated Diffuse and Coated Conductor Materials

Adding a dielectric interface on top of both diffuse materials and conductors is often useful to model surface reflection. For example, plastic can be modeled by putting such an interface above a diffuse material, and coated metals can be modeled by adding such an interface as well. In both cases, introducing a scattering layer can model effects like tarnish or weathering. Figure [14.22](#fig:layered-bxdf-dragon-renderings) shows the dragon model with a few variations of these.

pbrt provides both the CoatedDiffuseBxDF and the CoatedConductorBxDF for such uses. There is almost nothing to their implementations other than a public inheritance from [LayeredBxDF](#LayeredBxDF) with the appropriate types for the two interfaces.
